Refuge: A Collection of Urban Sanctuaries is a zine series exploring the historical evolution of urban green spaces in New York City, with a focus on their societal impact and modern cultural significance. The project covers Washington Square Park, Collect Pond Park, and the Brooklyn Heights Promenade. All zines and their contents were designed by Araceli Gonzalez-Rocha, as a part of her undergraduate thesis project. Urban sanctuaries are spaces within urban centers which provide a respite for individuals seeking solace from the chaos of the city. Often taking the form of parks, gardens, or green spaces, urban sanctuaries give people the opportunity to relax, contemplate, and recharge. They help build community and connection to the world around us.

A Brief History

Washington Square Park stands as one of NYC’s most renowned public parks, distinguished by its iconic Marble arch and fountain. Nestled within the heart of Greenwich Village on Manhattan’s Lower East Side, the park spans 9.75 acres, boasting a diverse array of amenities including seating areas, lush green lawns, playgrounds, dog runs, and paved walkways.
Long before European settlers arrived, the land now occupied by Washington Square Park was home to Native Americans of the Lenape Tribe, near a village known as Sapokanikan or “tobacco field.” The area featured marshy grounds and was traversed by Minetta Creek, a signifi- cant waterway that once flowed into the Hudson River. In 1624, the Dutch West India Company established a trading post in modern-day southern Manhattan. In 1632, the director of New Amsterdam gave land grants to a group of “half-freed” slaves in exchange for a portion of their crops to support the growing population at the time. This led to the establishment of an early African Amer- ican community known as the “Land of the Blacks” or “Little Africa” surrounding the park. The children of these former slaves were still born into slavery.
In 1797, the land was reacquired by the now English government in power and re-purposed into a potter’s field, a public burial ground, where the bodies of poor individuals and victims of the yellow fever epidemic were buried. It was rumored to also be a site for public executions, however, there are conflicting reports about where these executions took place. In 1825, the cemetery reached capacity and was closed, with an estimated 20,000 human remains still resting beneath the park’s surface to this day. Declaration of Independence, the land was designated as the “Washington Parade Ground,” and used for local militia training, before officially becoming a public park in 1827. With this change, affluent families moved into the area and built Greek Revival-style houses that still line the north side of the park, known as “The Row.” This resulted in a rise in the value of the surrounding real estate as well as increased desirability for living in the neighborhood.
Throughout its history, Washington Square Park has been the setting for significant historical events. In 1834, it became the site of one of New York’s first organized labor riots sparked by a dispute over the use of prison labor by New York University, then called the University of the City of New York. During the construction of its first university building on the east side of the park, NYU contracted prison laborers to complete the stone dressing of its building. The city’s stone cutters were infuriated by what they viewed as robbery from work that would have otherwise gone to them. This resulted in a violent riot that spread to nearby Astor Place and was only settled by the deployment of the local militia. In 1838, Samuel F.B. Morse demonstrated the revolutionizing invention of the telegraph for the first time in public within the park.
The City’s public parks department was established in 1870, prompting the redesign of the parade ground. M.A. Kel- logg, Engineer-in-Chief, and I.A. Pilat, Chief Landscape Gardener re-imagined the space according to Frederick Law Olmsted’s principles. This endeavor involved leveling the land, landscaping it, and introducing curvilinear paths and shade-providing trees.
In 1852, the first fountain was installed before being promptly replaced in 1872 by a fountain relocated from the southern entrance of Central Park at 59th Street. In 1889, a large wood arch

[page 7]

was constructed to commemorate the 100th anniversary of President George Washington’s inauguration. The arch proved to be a major success and architect Stanford White was commissioned to create a permanent arch made of marble, inspired by Paris’ Arc de Triomphe. The iconic Washington Square Arch was constructed between the years 1890 and 1892, and it now stands at an impressive 77 feet tall.
Throughout the 20th century, Washington Square Park emerged as a vibrant hub of social and cultural activity, drawing artists like street performers, musicians, and writers, among whom were notable figures such as Mark Twain and Allen Ginsberg. However, tragedy struck the park on March 25th, 1911, with the devastating fire at the nearby Triangle Shirtwaist Factory, claiming the lives of 146 young working-class immigrant women.
In 1935, Robert Moses, one of New York City’s most influential and controversial urban planners, proposed a renovation plan that would have obliterated the existing landscape by constructing a highway directly through the square, connecting Fifth Avenue to lower Manhattan. However, fierce neighborhood opposition resulted in a community victory, preventing the construction of the highway. During the 20th century, the park also became a focal point for civil rights protests, anti-war protests, and movements for social justice. Activists gathered in the park to rally, march, and organize, using its central location and symbolic significance to amplify their messages.
In 1964, driven by the advocacy of Greenwich Village residents, the park underwent a redesign, closing off traffic that previously flowed directly through the arch via Fifth Avenue.
Since then, Washington Square Park has undergone further enhancements, including the addition of playgrounds, chess tables, and performance plazas. Another multi-phase renovation project launched in 2009, aimed at improving accessibility, amenities, and overall aes- thetics, ensuring its continued role as a vibrant social hub in New York City.

Modern Day

In Washington Square Park, a dynamic mix of locals and tourists create an ever-changing scene. From artisan crafts, clothing, books, art, drugs, or impromptu poetry, independent street vendors offer an eclectic array of goods. Skateboarders glide through paved walkways and in the colder months fill the main fountain’s bowl. Visitors often lounge in the sun, surrounded by street performers, artists, NYU students, and families enjoying leisurely moments together. Children play, friends gather, and music fills the air, evoking a friendly atmosphere and positive energy throughout the park.
