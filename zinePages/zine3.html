Refuge: A Collection of Urban Sanctuaries is a zine series exploring the historical evolution of urban green spaces in New York City, with a focus on their societal impact and modern cultural significance. The project covers Washington Square Park, Collect Pond Park, and the Brooklyn Heights Promenade. All zines and their contents were designed by Araceli Gonzalez-Rocha, as a part of her undergraduate thesis project. The zines were printed using a risograph printer, and you can learn more about the process here.

Urban sanctuaries are spaces within urban centers which provide a respite for individuals seeking solace from the chaos of the city. Often taking the form of parks, gardens, or green spaces, urban sanctuaries give people the opportunity to relax, contemplate, and recharge. They help build community and connection to the world around us.

A Brief History

The Brooklyn Heights Promenade, also referred to as the Esplanade, is a pedestrian walkway and platform spanning 1,826 feet long, resting above the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway within the Brooklyn Heights neighborhood. It stretches from Remsen Street to Orange Street and offers unrivaled views of the lower Manhattan skyline and iconic landmarks like the Brooklyn Bridge, Statue of Liberty, and One World Trade Center. The area was originally a Native American settlement presumably called "Ihpetonga." Brooklyn Heights was initially settled by Europeans in 1645 and has since evolved into one of New York City's most desirable neighborhoods, conveniently located just a short subway ride from Manhattan. The neighborhood has a family-friendly and serene charm, boasting luxurious brownstone townhouses which in 2023 had a median cost of $1.19 million. During the Dutch colonial period in NYC, settlements were primarily concentrated

[page 5]

along Brooklyn's waterfront and bustling with ferry activities.
Following this period in the mid-1800s, the area where the Brooklyn Heights Promenade would be located was filled with mostly private gardens where locals frequently "promenaded." The original idea for the Promenade was first foreshadowed by a prominent landowner of Brooklyn Heights, Hezekiah Beers Pierrepont (1768-1838), During his time he proposed the idea of a walkway with gardens that allowed the residents of Brooklyn Heights to take in the amazing views of Manhattan. However, his ideas were not taken seriously.
It wasn't until 1941 when Robert Moses, a renowned urban planner and influential figure in New York City, introduced a proposal for a highway that could connect the Brooklyn and Queens boroughs. Ini- tially, Moses's plan involved cutting right through Brooklyn Heights, threatening the destruction of numerous historic structures.
Understandably, the local community was extremely opposed to these notions and fiercely lobbied for an alternative route. Community opposition compelled a reevaluation of plans, ultimately preserving the neighborhood's architectural heritage. The alternative proposal put forth and eventually passed shifted the highway so that it would run along the neighborhood's edge.

(Image: Pierrepont St & Henry St, Mar. 29, 2024.)

[page 6]

Construction on the highway commenced in 1946 and was conclud- ed in 1954. However, the southern section of the Brooklyn Promenade opened to the public on October 7th, 1950, followed by the northern section on December 7th, 1951.
Fast forward to the present, the Brooklyn Heights Promenade faces a different kind of challenge - structural wear from relentless traffic. Proposed solutions include a six-year closure of the Promenade for a six-lane highway, estimated to cost $3.6 billion dollars The alternative is an eight-year repair plan for the highway which would see the Prom- enade closed for around 2 years and would total $4 billion dollars. As discussions unfold, the community remains engaged in shaping the future of this iconic landmark. It's unclear what the plans for repairing the Brooklyn Promenade are but options are still being considered.
This story of the Brooklyn Heights Promenade stands as a testament to community activism and unity prevailing against the destruction of a neighborhood's history and culture by government officials who disregard local heritage and sentiments.

MY FRESHMAN YEAR OF COLLEGE I LIVED ON CLARK ST at NYU's dorm in Brooklyn Heights. I was a broke college kid living in a shared studio with a roommate, surrounded by fancy brownstone townhouses probably owned by millionaires. The juxtaposition made me laugh. I was entranced by NYC and how surreal it felt to live there, after only knowing my small hometown in the middle of Florida. Every night, I went to the Brooklyn Promenade and sat on one of the benches surrounding a globe sculpture there. It was around a seven minute walk from my dorm. It became a nightly ritual. During the day, the Brooklyn Promenade is usually busy. I would see a lot of tourists, families taking a stroll, people going on runs, or dog walkers. But at night, I was lucky to see more than five people the whole night. Sometimes I saw no one. I had the whole space to myself. Thousands of people probably live in Brooklyn Heights, but not many had the same idea as me. I still remember the dark navy blue sky contrasted against the white city lights. I felt blessed every night to have a view as beautiful as that.
